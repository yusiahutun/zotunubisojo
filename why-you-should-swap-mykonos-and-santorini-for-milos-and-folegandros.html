Why you should swap Mykonos and Santorini for Milos and Folegandros

Posted by Martina Birk on Tuesday, May 7, 2024

In a country that has 6,000 islands, it’s a little surprising that we only ever seem to hear about a handful of them. And sure enough, in a country with countless ancient treasures to tempt visitors with, Mykonos and Santorini still attract more tourist footfall than anywhere else in Greece. Unsurprisingly, the other islands are often as stunning as their supermodel sister Santorini – and you’ll get the added bonus of feeling wholesome at not contributing to over-tourism.

Sarakiniko © Discover Greece

Milos is the richest of the Cyclades, in more ways than one – mining has created a well-heeled population, and the landscape of this butterfly-shaped island is spectacularly varied (geologists will have a field day; literally). The island is mineral rich from its volcanic activity (also the reason for those red, pink and orange beaches); and it’s responsible for a significant amount of the world’s bentonite and perlite needs. It is already popular with American tourists (including Madonna and Tom Hanks) and the influencers have been steadily incoming for the past few years – if Sarakiniko isn’t the definition of “Instagram worthy” then I don’t know what is – but if that usually makes you want to run a mile in the opposite direction, bear with me. The most common thing people like to tell you about Milos is that it’s where the famous Venus de Milo statue was found, though there’s a lot more to uncover here, too.

Milos © Caroline Lewis

What to do in Milos
Head to Klima to see the charming, colourful line up of former fisherman’s houses along the water, now a series of shops or adventurous Airbnbs. The island has countless kaleidoscopic beaches on the edge of clear turquoise waters – for the best viewpoint, jump in a boat and cruise around the island with Captain Mike of Excellent Yachting. He’ll circumnavigate Milos for you, stopping off at Polyegos (fun fact: the name means many goats), which is the biggest uninhabited (bar the multitudinous wild goats) island in the Med. You’ll also call in at Kleftiko, Fyrligos and Glaronisia to admire the rock formations, dropping anchor for swims when you fancy, and spy Sarakiniko from the water.

Or hike the island’s lunar landscape on a Travel Me to Milos walking tour, where you’ll conquer the blinding-white rocks of Sarakiniko and explore the Papafragkas cave. If you’re brave (or just able to hike in high temperatures), make the descent by ladder to Tsigrado on the southern coast, most famous for being particularly tricky to reach – but worth it to have your own private paradise when you get there (possibly worth taking the yacht for this one). At the end of the day, head up to the ancient capital, Plaka, high on a hill for pirate-evading purposes and with a 13th-century Venetian fort – it gives Santorini a run for its money in terms of showtime sunsets.

Klima © Caroline Lewis

Where to stay
This is a leave-your-doors-open kind of place, where the islanders treat you like some long-lost relative. Captain Zeppos Boutique Hotel is a small-scale retreat with just a handful of suites and one villa in the heart of Pollonia. The owner can arrange car hire from right outside, as well as kayaks to borrow. When I was there, a trio of American dudes walked by the pool terrace and came up the steps to ask the owner how much it would cost to borrow his kayaks for the afternoon. He soon set them up and was waving them off, with no question of any money being exchanged. Down the road in Pollonia, Del Mar Apartment & Suites is another boutique hotel with a similar friendly set-up, where studio-style suites have whitewashed walls, raftered ceilings and handy kitchenettes (and the exterior has the winning Greek combination of bougainvillea-draped bright white walls). There's also a terrace to enjoy your breakfast on and the beach is only 50 metres away. The concierge can help arrange sailing, hiking and diving trips.

Kleftiko © Municipality of Milos

Where to eat
Unsurprisingly, seafood is the order of the day and it doesn’t get better (or fresher) than this. In Pollonia, superb seafood and expertly matched wines await at Armenaki. And a few doors down, don’t miss the salt-baked catch of the day at Armira. At the family-run taverna O Hamos, down by the port, sit out on the garden terrace and choose from the handwritten menu that’s been painstakingly translated into nine languages (if they go to that much effort over the menu, you can imagine what the food’s like). It’s traditional and typical of the island, with house-made cheese, home-grown veg and lots of lamb and goat dishes. You’ll also be able to scribble on the walls in a marker pen if you like (a more direct way of leaving a review).

Folegandros
Folegandros is tiny, sleepy and much more like what your average Greek island (even Mykonos and Santorini) used to be several decades ago. The petite island has a population of around 700 in winter, which does swell in the summer, but nowhere near as much as its neighbours’ does. A pilgrimage to Panagia sees all of the islanders come together every Easter. The mayor ensures preservation measures are in place – such as banning all electric signage, in favour of authentic hand-painted ones. No-one’s in a rush on Folegandros. The islanders will gladly stop and chat to you; they’re not just trying to make a buck.

What to do in Folegandros
It won’t take you long to drive from one end of the island to the other, and it’s easy to see its sights in a day – which makes it excellent for proper relaxation potential. The zig-zagging hike up to Panagia will earn you a rest afterwards. The best beach is probably Katergo, and a handy free boat shuttle goes back and forth to it from the main port throughout the day.

A suite at Anemi in Folegandros

Where to stay
The island may have a back-in-time feel, but at Anemi all the mod-cons are present and correct. This boutique hotel is handily placed right by the port, with a sleek pool terrace, villa-style suites and breakfasts that will set you up for the hike up to Chora’s Panagia church.

Where to eat
In Chora, evenings are busy, with locals and tourists congregating at outdoor taverns like Zefiros Anemos, where you can enjoy just-caught seafood in a peaceful garden setting. For a taste of the island’s national dish – matsata pasta, an heirloom entrusted by Italian immigrants – Sinadisi in Ano Meria does it best. And for something smart, book a table on the terrace of Blue Cuisine in Chora and enjoy the traditional Greek plates with a fine-dining twist.

Book it
